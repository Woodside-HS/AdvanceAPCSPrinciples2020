Encryption type: Caesar Cipher

Caesar ciphers are one of the oldest and most widely-used forms of cryptology. In it, the alphabet is shifted down a certain number of letters, either left or right. With a right shift of 2, A would become C, B would become D, and so on. Made famous by Caesar, they offer almost no security in the modern day.

Encryption type: Vigenère Cipher

In this cipher, named after Blaise de Vigenère yet invented by Giovan Battista Bellaso, is much more simple than it seems. In it, one writes out the alphabet, shifts it down one letter, then writes it again, looping and creating a table. At the top axis of the table, the word or phrase to be encrypted is used. On the left axis, the keyword is used (and repeated to be the correct length). Where the two intersect, (i.e. the first letter of the "real" word and the first letter of the keyword), the new letter is used.

Encryption type: Public Key Encryption

Public key encryption, invented (in part) by my neighbor Whitfield (who happens to have a very cute dog), is a secure method of encryption using pairs of keys, one public key and one private used only by the owner. Using one-way functions it can be made entirely secure.
